About the Species

Introduction

A tufted titmouse is a small bird found in North America, a species falling in the tit and Chickadee family.
The genus name "Baeolophus" in simple terms means a 'small crested' and is formed of fusion of two Ancient Greek words. The term bicolor means two-colored.

Descriptives:

Measurements:

  • Length: 14-16 cm
  • Wingspan: 20-26 cm
  • Weight: 17-26 grams

These birds have a white front, and grey upper body with rust coloured flanks. They also have a tufted, grey crest on their heads, and a black forehead. The black forehead is notably reduced in youngsters, making them easily mistaken for oak titmice. In general, males are bigger than females.

Chirp:

These birds usually sing something similar to a whistled peter-peter-peter, although it can vary and hence has 20 different versions of it.

Habitats and Distribution:

Its natural habitat includes shrublands, gardens, parks, and mixed and deciduous woodlands. The tufted titmouse is a non-migratory bird that used to be found only in the Ohio and Mississippi River basins. However, due to factors like bird feeders, it has spread its range across the United States and into Ontario and Quebec in Canada. The species' territory has been extending farther north over the second part of the 20th century and into the 21st.

Food:

Insects such as caterpillars, beetles, ants, wasps, stink bugs, treehoppers, spiders, and snails are the main summertime food of tufted titmice. Acorns and beech nuts are some of the seeds, nuts, and berries that Tufted Titmice feed on. Tufted Titmice studies revealed that they always pick the biggest seeds available when foraging.

Nesting:

Although Tufted Titmice can nest in cavities, they are unable to dig them out on their own. They make advantage of the natural openings and abandoned nest cavities left behind by a variety of woodpecker species, even big ones like the Pileated Woodpecker and Northern Flicker. Tufted Titmice also build their nests in metal pipes, fenceposts, and nest boxes made of man-made materials.

How does a nest of these Birds look like?

Titmice use damp leaves, moss and grasses, and strips of bark to build cup-shaped nests inside the nest hole. They sometimes remove hairs straight from living animals to line this cup, lining it with soft materials like hair, fur, wool, and cotton. Raccoon, opossum, dog, fox squirrel, red squirrel, rabbit, horse, cow, cat, mouse, woodchuck, and even human hair have been found in titmouse nests by naturalists who have examined old nests. It usually takes 6 to 11 days to build a nest.

SOME FACTS ABOUT TUFTED TITMICE

  • The oldest Tufted Titmouse in the wild ever found lived at least 13 years, 3 months old. In Virginia, it was found in 1962, and in the same state, it was discovered in 1974.

  • At times, the Black-crested Titmouse of Texas and Mexico has been mistaken for a Tufted Titmouse. When the two species meet, hybridization occurs, but the zone is small and stable over time. They exhibit genetic variations as well as a minor difference in call quality.

Behaviour:

Tufted Titmice look for food by flying from branch to branch of the forest canopy, frequently in the company of other birds like nuthatches, chickadees, kinglets, and woodpeckers. Titmice normally grasp the seed with their feet and crack it open with their beaks when they encounter large seeds, like the sunflower seeds they collect from bird feeders. They often store these shelled seeds in bark cracks during the fall and winter. These acrobatic foragers frequently hang sideways or upside down while they inspect cones, branch undersides, and leaf clusters. They may descend completely to the ground to walk around in hunt of scattered seeds or insects.

Conservation and Population Numbers:

According to the North American Breeding Bird Survey, Tufted Titmice numbers have grown between 1966 and 2019 which makes them common. According to Partners in Flight, there are 12 million mating birds worldwide, and the species receives a Continental Concern Score of 7, which is the lowest possible score. Over the past 50 years, their range has been moving north. The rising temperatures, farmlands turning back into woods, and the rising popularity of home bird feeders are a few potential causes of range extension.
